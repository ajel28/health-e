Measures of Your Physique

  • Body Mass Index (BMI)

    • Body mass index (BMI) is a medical screening tool that measures the ratio of your height to your weight to estimate the amount of body fat you have. Healthcare providers calculate BMI by using weight in kilograms (kg) divided by the square of height in meters (m2).

    In most people, BMI correlates to body fat — the higher the number, the more body fat you may have — but it’s not accurate in some cases. BMI alone does not diagnose health. Healthcare providers use BMI and other tools and tests to assess someone’s health status and risks.

    You should not use the standard BMI chart to evaluate a child’s or teenager’s weight

  • Body Fat Percentage

    • Body fat percentage measures the proportion of fat in your body relative to your overall weight. While BMI provides a general health indicator, body fat percentage offers a more precise assessment of body composition.

    Physiologically, women require higher essential body fat than men for overall health. Factors such as age and sex also influence an individual’s ideal body fat percentage. For most males, a healthy range typically falls between 14-20% and females 20-30%, depending on these variables.

    There are various methods to measure body fat, but even the most advanced techniques come with significant margins of error.

  • Body Measurements

    • Taking body measurements is an optimal way to track weight progress because you get an idea of what's really happening with your body. Knowing how to take body measurements is a valuable tool if you're trying to change your body composition by losing fat and/or adding muscle. You should take body measurements every 2-4 weeks.

    For the most consistent results, there are some things to ensure you are doing when taking body measurements. You should either wear fitted clothing or no clothing at all so that the garments do not factor into the measurements. When taking body measurements yourself, stand tall with your feet together, and stay relaxed without tensing, flexing, or sucking in your stomach.

    Your body loses fat all over, but the areas that hold excess fat take longer. The bottom line is, you can't control where the fat comes off, but you can look at your own body type and that of your parents and get a decent idea of where you tend to store more fat and where you don't.

    Important areas of the body to measure are your abs, arms, calves, chest, hips, thighs, and waist
