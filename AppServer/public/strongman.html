Strongman

  • What is Strongman?

    • Strongman is a weightlifting-based sport where athletes compete in a series of events that test various aspects of mental and physical strength, speed, and endurance. The wide range of events allows the sport to accommodate athletes of different body types and skill sets. Some competitions prioritize sheer size and raw power, with extremely heavy weights, while others focus on endurance and agility, requiring competitors to move moderately heavy loads over a set distance or within a time limit.

    In many well-balanced competitions, success depends on a combination of strength, speed, and overall physical conditioning, making it a versatile and dynamic sport. Strongman athletes must not only be able to lift massive weights but also demonstrate exceptional mobility, endurance, and mental toughness, adapting to the diverse challenges each competition presents.

  • Workout Split

    • There are multiple workout splits a strongman can follow. A reminder is that all of these splits should be conducted with exercises that promote strength and endurance in the muscles. Here are some examples:

    Push; Pull; Legs/Abs - These days should follow each other concurrently (in any order), with at least one-two days of rest in between every cycle.

    Chest/Biceps/Triceps; Back/Forearms/Shoulders; Legs/Abs - These days should follow each other concurrently (in any order), with at least one-two days of rest in between every cycle.

    Chest; Back; Arms; Legs; Abs (Bro Split) - These days should follow each other concurrently (in any order), with at least one-two days of rest in between every cycle.

  • Body Composition

    • For strongmen, the ideal body composition emphasizes strength and power over aesthetics, with a focus on maximizing muscle mass and functional performance. Strongmen typically have higher body fat percentages than bodybuilders, as extra weight can provide a mechanical advantage in lifting heavy loads. Male strongmen usually maintain a body fat percentage of around 15% to 25%, while female strongwomen may range from 20% to 30%. This additional body fat helps with stability, endurance, and overall strength during events like deadlifts, overhead presses, and carrying heavy objects.

Strongmen prioritize heavy, compound lifts, often using lower rep ranges to increase maximal strength. Nutrition plays a key role, with a focus on high-calorie, protein-rich meals to fuel intense training and promote recovery. A well-balanced diet that includes ample carbohydrates, fats, and proteins helps maintain the energy needed for both training and competition. Strongman athletes thrive on a balance of mass, strength, and power, using their body composition to excel in strength-based challenges.

  • Pros

    • Strongman training offers a variety of benefits, including weight loss, improved cardiovascular fitness, increased muscle mass, and enhanced functional strength. Functional strength refers to the ability to move heavy or awkward objects in real-world scenarios, a central focus of strongman training. This also requires conditioning, as being strong without stamina limits the effectiveness of the strength gained. Strongman exercises often involve lifting, carrying, and pulling heavy loads, helping to develop not just strength but also endurance and mobility.

    Additionally, strongman training helps overcome plateaus by introducing new and unfamiliar movements that stimulate further progress. It promotes muscle growth and bone density by keeping muscles under tension for extended periods, which is especially beneficial for the elderly. Key exercises also target trunk and grip strength, crucial for stability and daily tasks. Activities like sled pulls challenge cardiovascular fitness by forcing the body to deliver oxygen and blood to large muscle groups over long distances, boosting endurance and overall fitness.

  • Cons

    • While strongman training offers numerous benefits, there are also potential health risks to consider. The heavy lifting and high-intensity nature of the sport can increase the risk of injury, particularly to the joints, tendons, and ligaments. Overuse injuries, such as strains or sprains, are common due to the repetitive, high-load movements involved. Additionally, the extreme stress placed on the body during intense events can lead to overtraining, which may cause fatigue, decreased performance, and increased susceptibility to illness.

    Another consideration is the potential for cardiovascular strain, particularly in novice athletes who are not accustomed to the intense physical demands of strongman training. The high-impact movements and heavy lifting can put undue stress on the heart, especially for those with pre-existing heart conditions. Furthermore, the focus on maximizing strength and muscle mass can lead to an imbalance in overall fitness, neglecting other important aspects of health, such as flexibility and aerobic conditioning. Proper technique, adequate rest, and balanced training are essential to minimizing these risks.
